Art Basel Hong Kong, 2026

A cube started
100,000 conversations.

Work, Luck, Play was a three-part exhibition that used the cube as its central form factor. Sterling silver dice. Transparent resin dice. A live trading mechanic that turned an art fair into a game.

Each piece used the same 19mm cube. The material and the rules changed everything.

Part I

Work

Four sterling silver hand sculptures cast at Asprey Studio, London. Each traces labor's migration from physical craft to digital abstraction. A sculpture about labor's disappearance, made through intense visible labor.

Part II

Luck

Six sterling silver dice. Every face on every die shows the same number. Roll a six, get six. Always. Certainty engines disguised as probability tools.

Part III

Play

Transparent resin dice in randomized sets. Roll at the booth, receive your set, trade across the fair to complete 1–6. The game was the art. The strangers were the medium.

The mechanic that made it work.

01

Visitor arrives at the booth. Rolls a standard pair of dice.

02

Receives a set of transparent resin dice corresponding to their roll — randomized, not sequential.

03

To complete the 1–6 sequence, they find other participants across the fair and trade face to face.

04

Return a complete set to the booth. Receive a signed edition. Or keep whatever collection you end up with.

A parallel mechanic ran with Starbucks — numbered coasters distributed at the booth and participating stores. Collect all six, return to the booth, roll for a custom resin set. 750 signed sets. All claimed.

What it proved.

The object did the work

No instructions needed. People saw a cube, picked it up, and interacted. The form factor has a built-in call to action.

Material changed the meaning

Sterling silver said permanence. Transparent resin said play. Same geometry. Completely different emotional register.

Trading created community

The incomplete set was the engine. Having three out of six meant you needed three strangers. The fair became a game board.

Brands plugged in naturally

Starbucks integrated through the mechanic, not the messaging. The format accepted the collaboration without distortion.

People kept them

Incomplete sets, single dice, coasters — participants held onto everything. Not because of resale value. Because the object was a souvenir of a conversation they wouldn't have had otherwise.

Why this shape

Six reasons the cube is the
collectible form factor.

01

It fits the hand

The simplest three-dimensional object that feels intentional to hold. Coins are flat. Spheres roll away. A cube sits, stacks, stays.

02

Six faces

Every cube is a tiny gallery. Each face can carry a different message, mark, or edition number. No other form at this scale gives you six distinct canvases.

03

It stacks

Cubes compose. One is an object. Two is a pair. Six is a set. A shelf of them is a collection. They self-organize into grids.

04

Universal shorthand

Dice, building blocks, sugar cubes, Rubik's cubes. The form is pre-loaded with cultural meaning. It reads as game, toy, art, and material simultaneously.

05

Conversation piece

Most collectibles sit behind glass. A cube gets passed around. The object creates a reason to interact.

06

Production friendly

Cubes cast well, mold well, machine well. Sterling silver, resin, wood, concrete, glass, titanium. The form translates across every material.

The Model

The constraint
is the brand.

One shape. Every variable changes except the shape. Material is the message. A concrete cube and a sterling silver cube say different things. Both are unmistakably Cube.

The cube is the brand identity. Everything else is variable. This is the opposite of most collectible brands that try to do everything. The constraint makes it legible.

The Volume

The cube has an inside.

Six faces is the surface story. But a cube is also a volume. An enclosure. A vitrine at palm scale.

Cast in clear resin or glass, the cube becomes the display case and the collectible simultaneously. An object suspended inside is permanently preserved, permanently presented.

A fragment of material. A component. A miniature. Sand from a specific place. A seed. A chip. A pressed flower. The thing inside gives the cube its meaning. The cube gives the thing inside its permanence.

The cube is the frame.
What's inside is the art.
